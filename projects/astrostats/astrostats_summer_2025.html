GP-based Imputation in
Kepler Stellar Light Curves

Independent Research · 19-page manuscript

↓ Download Paper (PDF) GitHub — Simulations GitHub — Research Repo (Private)

Overview

This independent research project follows my participation in the AstroStatistics summer school at Penn State University. The work develops a Gaussian Process framework for imputing missing observations in Kepler stellar photometric time series.

Kepler light curves are affected by instrumental gaps, safe-mode events, and scheduled downtime that result in missing data. Classical deterministic interpolation approaches fail to respect the correlated, non-stationary nature of stellar flux. This study applies GP regression with kernels tuned to specific stellar variability timescales.

Methods

The study evaluates two specific regimes: a quiet solar-like star with stochastic scatter, and an active star exhibiting quasi-periodic rotational modulation. For each dataset, appropriate covariance kernels are selected (squared-exponential and quasi-periodic respectively) and hyperparameters are fitted by marginal likelihood maximisation.

The GP imputation procedure produces posterior predictive distributions over missing segments, enabling principled uncertainty quantification rather than point-fill imputation.

Highlights

  • Posterior predictive coverage analysis across quiet and rotating stellar profiles.
  • Comparison against deterministic linear interpolation and ARIMA(1,0,2) baselines.
  • Implementation of GP-based probabilistic imputation and uncertainty quantification on Kepler observations.
  • Investigation of Change-Point Gaussian Process (CP-GP) models for shifting variance regimes.

Key Findings

  • Gaussian Processes and ARIMA achieved nearly identical predictive accuracy.
  • Predictive uncertainty exhibited near-ideal probabilistic calibration.
  • Stationary quasi-periodic kernels struggled with evolving rotational variability.
  • Results suggest a principled need for non-stationary GP formulations.
